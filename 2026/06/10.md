## Words don't mean in ads what they mean in English

> If the front of supplement bottle says "supports X" or "promotes Y," that should be a signal to you that it is not actually proven to do anything.

[Why is there a supplement craze if they don’t even work?]

This is true of every ad. If you see general platitudes on home listings like "great neighborhood," "gorgeous," or, heaven forbid, an exclamation point, that means the house itself has nothing going for it, nothing the realtor could put into the review as a positive reason to buy it. _Freakonomics_ taught me this. So, I shouldn't have been surprised to learn that there is particular language on pill bottles that signifies to lawyers that its benefits aren't meant to be taken seriously, at least not in court. No one can sue a salesman for a supplement that promises to support metabolism but doesn't actually burn its taker's fat.
