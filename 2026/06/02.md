## A fungal colony merges with an algal colony to live more efficiently as a lichen

<img width="400" alt="overview_image_56812304_397344" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/e8388d8f-c740-4b15-bffd-22380dc32766" />

I passed by a tree with a fungal colony growing in its bark. The colony was so enormous it caught my eye and made me curious. How could something like this grow here? Is the tree so sick that it allowed a parasite to grow so much? How can this fungal colony withstand the UV radiation from the sun shining on it so many hours every day? As I came to find out, the tree is not sick, the fungus uses its bark as a substrate (simply uses its surface as something to grow on rather than growing into the tree), and it is not really a fungus at all, at least not entirely. It is an organism that includes both fungal and algal cells, a lichen (specifically, [powdery axil-bristle lichen], _Myelochroa aurulenta_). Could this be what saves it from getting fatally damaged by UV radiation? Algal cells perform photosynthesis, so they must have evolved capacity for greater exposure to sunlight.

## Lichen

> A composite organism that emerges from algae or cyanobacteria living among the filaments (hyphae) of the fungi in a mutually beneficial symbiotic relationship. ... A hybrid colony of algae or cyanobacteria living symbiotically among filaments of multiple fungus species, along with bacteria embedded in the cortex, or "skin", in a mutualistic relationship. Lichens are the lifeform that first brought the term symbiosis (as _Symbiotismus_) into biological context.

[Lichen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lichen).

Next time you see two colonies fighting it out for territory, it's possible that they may be evolving not new weapons (substances toxic to the other, like antibiotics) but rather a symbiotic new species. It apparently happened when a fungal colony fought an algal colony but eventually it grew into it and formed a new kind of organism, a lichen, which was more successful than the individual colonies by themselves.

## Trophallaxis

> The direct transfer of fluid and food (excreted, secreted or regurgitated) between individuals, typically through mouth-to-mouth (stomodeal) or anus-to-mouth (proctodeal) feeding. Along with nutrients, trophallaxis can involve the transfer of molecules such as proteins, hormones, organisms such as symbionts, and information to serve as a form of communication.

[Trophallaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trophallaxis).

One spring morning I woke up to an ant infestation. As I watched the ants run across my wall, I noticed that one of them ran up to another, gave it a long kiss right on the mouth, and ran away. They're just like us, aren't they? Except they aren't that much like us. They don't kiss for affection. They kiss to regurgitate their food and water. That's how nutrients travel across the composite organism that is the ant colony.
