## Paving the desire paths 

> A design principle from physical spaces like parks and campuses. Landscape architects look for places where people have worn the grass thin by cutting across lawns and fields (desire paths) and formalize them by leveling them and paving them or putting down gravel, wood chips, or some other material. ... Workarounds were observed and turned into official features.

[Enshittification].

There's no such thing as being overly obsessed with usage data. That's your most honest window into what your users want out of your product. If they create workarounds, convert them into features. If many of them perform the same sequence of actions, collapse those into a single action. Optimize your user experience based on what you observe in their interactions with your product. There's no need to ask your users what they want if you already have detailed usage data on your product. Revealed preferences are more important than declared preferences. 

## Capitalism turns greed into productivity, but this doesn't work without constraints

> Once a company is too big to fail, it becomes too big to jail, and then it becomes too big to care. Antitrust law was designed to fight that apathy and force companies to care.

[Enshittification].

Capitalism works so well because it doesn't try to go against human nature. Humans are greedy, but instead of moralizing on the subject, it puts it to use. But greed really is immoral, and if it's not constrained, it could lead to society's downfall. So, if you want capitalism to function, you need regulation. Antitrust law, privacy regulations, security compliance, things like that. Give companies the guardrails to push against, otherwise they will fall off the rails and drag all of society along with them. So get them busy bringing their products to compliance. Distract them from their greed.
