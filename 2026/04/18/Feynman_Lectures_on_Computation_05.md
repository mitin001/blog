# 5. Reversible Computation

This chapter of [Feynman Lectures on Computation](../../../2025/12/05/Feynman_Lectures_on_Computation.md) answered a question about a biological reversible reaction.

## What is preventing the RNA polymerase from moving the opposite way and chipping the bases off the RNA transcript it is otherwise assembling?

The direction of the RNA polymerase's movement depends on the concentration of its reactants vs. products in its vicinity.

> If there is an excess of free pyrophosphates over triphosphates, the reaction will tend to run the wrong way, and we’ll be uncopying and ruining our copy. We can actually interpret these relative concentrations in terms of the number of possible states available to our system at any given computational point. If there are plenty of triphosphates around, then there are plenty of forward-moving, and comparatively few backward-moving, states available: the RNA polymerase will tend to enter the former state, in the process lowering its entropy. The difference in free energies, mea­sured by the differing concentrations, determines the way it goes.

The cell keeps the concentration of the RNA polymerization products low: the pyrophosphates are quickly removed by hydrolysis.
