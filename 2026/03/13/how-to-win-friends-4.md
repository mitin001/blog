# Part Four. Be a Leader: How to Change People Without Giving Offense or Arousing Resentment

This part of [How to Win Friends and Influence People](../../../2026/01/19/how-to-win-friends.md) answered three questions about smart leadership.

## Why is it a bad idea to badmouth your leaders? 

Not for fear of retaliation, but because this poisons the morale of your team. A smart leader will find such acts of internal sabotage and try to reason with the saboteur. Abraham Lincoln was one such smart leader. He wrote a letter to General Hooker in which he criticized him for his attempts at poisoning the morale of his troops.

> I much fear that the spirit that you have aided to infuse into the army, of criticizing their commander and withholding confidence from him, will now turn upon you. I shall assist you, as far as I can, to put it down.

If you disagree with your leaders, don't undermine them by getting a few people to pull against them and the team. This will slow them down somewhat, but the team will still be pulling in the direction that seems wrong to you, except there will also be hurt feelings. Instead, talk to your leaders and try to get them to pull in what you see as the right direction. Perhaps, they can convince you that the direction you want to take the team is the wrong direction.

## What to do when you catch someone cutting corners at work? 

Write them a letter pointing out the specific areas of their work that have been lacking, except don't frame it in an accusatory way. Frame it as if the missed requirements were add-ons and promise to pay them a premium for them.

> My dear Martin, I see you so seldom, so I thought I’d take the time to thank you for the fine job of cleaning you’ve been doing. By the way, I thought I’d mention that since two hours, twice a week, is a very limited amount of time, please feel free to work an extra half hour from time to time if you feel you need to do those “once-in-a-while” things like polishing the cup holders and the like. I, of course, will pay you for the extra time.

This level of care toward a subordinate will signal to him that he needs not feel any tension at work. This will give him a comfortable room to grow into someone who meets and exceeds expectations. Show a person that you've entrusted him with something important, and he will do almost anything not to risk forsaking this trust. This is especially true of an excellent worker who, starved out of praise and caught up in the monotony, has let his work slip.

> Mr. Henke had a good mechanic whose work had been less than satisfactory of late. It had become slipshod and not completed in time. But instead of bawling him out or threatening him, Mr. Henke called him into his office for a heart-to-heart talk. “Bill,” he said, “you are a fine mechanic. You have been in this line of work for a good number of years. You have repaired many vehicles to the customers’ satisfaction. In fact, we’ve had a number of compliments about the good work you have done. Yet, of late, the time you take to complete each job has been increasing and your work has not been up to your own standards. Because you have been such an outstanding mechanic in the past, I felt sure you would want to know that I am not happy with this situation, and perhaps jointly we could find some way to correct the problem.” Bill responded that he hadn’t realized he had been falling down in his duties and assured his boss that the work he was getting was not out of his range of expertise and he would try to improve in the future. Did he do it? You can be sure he did. He once again became a fast and thorough mechanic. With that reputation Mr. Henke had given him to live up to, how could he do anything else but turn out work comparable to that which he had done in the past? So if you want to influence a person in a certain respect, act as though that particular trait was already one of his or her outstanding characteristics. Shakespeare said, “Assume a virtue, if you have it not.” And it might be well to assume and state openly that the other party has the virtue that you want them to develop. Give them a fine reputation to live up to and they will make prodigious efforts rather than see you disillusioned.

## How to get someone to do something? 

Frame your request in such a way that they immediately see what's in it for them. Frame it from their point of view and with their interests at the forefront.

> It is only a matter of finding a way for others to get something out of helping us; recognition, appreciation, or a reward for their cooperation.

Everyone aspires to act in their best interest and no one else's. If you make your interests aligned (e.g., allow your worker to get a thing he desperately seeks—like the feeling of importance—out of the work _you_ need done), then you will effectively be proposing a win-win arrangement, something that very few people seem to be smart enough to strike.
